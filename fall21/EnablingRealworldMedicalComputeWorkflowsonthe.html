Project Overview:

Background:  The ChRIS project -- a container based scheduler with a UI tailored for

computation typical in medical science -- started at Boston Children's

Hospital and has since grown into an active and on-going collaboration

between BU/NEU/Red Hat/MOC/BCH.

Unlike many similar container/compute frameworks, ChRIS is:

* pervasively open source

* passionately committed to open science / open data

* focused on *compute* rather than *data* (most existing solutions have one-step compute from data to results and strongly specify the data formats/sharing/managing). ChRIS focuses on multi-step compute and is unopinionated on data formats, structures, etc.

Project Specifics:  The use of software to advance medicine in a practical sense is still

relatively young. While medicine (typially in First World countries) routinely uses sophisticated devices (MRIs/CTs/etc), these devices are often little more than "dumb" data aggregators. Ultimately they measure some information and then simply show that information to a human expert, often in the same physical location as the device. The interpretation is still left overwhelmingly to a human when in many cases software (not even "AI", just plain old statistics) can provide lots of value.

Using software to better database/analyze/synthesize this wealth of data can empower patients, doctors, and researchers in ways that currently are hard or almost impossible to do. A patient in Brazil can get a second opinion (from another doctor, or a software analysis) by logging in and sharing her data. A Radiology department can reduce the variation in diagnoses across different doctors. An urgent care provider in Uganda can upload a lung CT to a ChRIS instance running in Australia and get a first pass probability of COVID-19 infection.  Researchers can access complex data and experiment with new algorithms, discover new solutions, and get these solutions back to clinical workflows easily.

While most of those goals remain a distance away yet, the ChRIS project is actively engaged in enabling this.

In this project, we will focus on providing and improving the clinical utility of an existing workflow that already runs in ChRIS. This workflow, brain image classification, already runs on kubernetes and openshift.

The actual specifics of the project are somewhat elastic, since this will depend on the group's skills and interests, but as a proposal we might take an existing workflow that uses a Neural Network to quickly segment a brain image (that means classifying each part of the image as belonging to a specific tissue) and currently generates a simple report of results.

Ideally we would like to interface the ChRIS platform to also support other "backends" such as a standard High Performance Compute setup using slurm (as is typical in compute centers at universities). Note HPC is both different and similar to "cloud". This will require understanding both cloud schedulers, and then also HPC schedulers and coding the HPC ability into ChRIS microservices.

Depending on group interests and abilities, we might also improve the reporting that a doctor will see on a workflow. We might expand the workflow that takes the results of one analysis and provides a metric of how "close" to normal these results are. We might improve/expand the webUI, or add new compute "nodes" to the workflow.
